ABC/XYZ Cycle Count Framework

A Tier-Top 80% revenue
B Tier-Next 15% revenue
C Tier-Bottom 5% revenue
X-CV ≤ 3.96 (catalog 25th percentile)
Y-CV > 3.96 and ≤ 10.91 (middle 50%)
Z-CV > 10.91 (top 25%)

Project source: A one-page reference for how ABC/XYZ classification assigns cycle count schedules in the Power BI project howardchungnyc/abc-xyz-segmentation-inventory-management. For this catalog, X and Y share the same count schedule on every A, B, and C row. Z counts more often due to its unpredictable demand. Roughly 81% of this catalog’s SKUs average under one unit sold per day, so day-to-day volume is too low for X and Y to require different real-world counting schedules. Only Z carries enough demand volatility to justify stepping the schedule up. See decision log Entry #22 in the linked repository’s docs.

CV calibration: X, Y, and Z band each SKU by how much daily unit sales fluctuate relative to that SKU’s average daily volume (coefficient of variation = standard deviation of daily sales ÷ average daily sales). The legend lists this catalog’s CV thresholds. They are not generic industry defaults or a standard 0–1 textbook CV band.

Three ways to read each ABC/XYZ combination:

ABC Lens
Opportunity cost of a stockout. Higher tier = larger revenue at risk per error.
Safety Stock Lens
Buffer against demand variability and lead time variance between replenishments.
XYZ Lens
Record accuracy risk. Higher variability = errors accumulate and hide longer between counts.
AX
Count: Monthly
A-High Revenue X-Stable
ABC-Stockout Exposure
High: A miscounted A item puts top-line revenue at risk. Any gap between system and physical directly threatens fill rate on your highest-contribution SKU.
Safety Stock
Modest: Stable demand means safety stock is sized primarily for lead time variance, not demand swings. Reorder points are reliable and consistent.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
Low: Predictable movement makes discrepancies visible through normal operations. Errors are unlikely to compound before the next count.
Scenario
System shows 600 units on hand. A receiving error logged 50 units that never arrived. Because weekly movement is consistent at ~500 units, the shelf looks light within days and the error surfaces before replenishment is affected.
AY
Count: Monthly
A-High Revenue Y-Moderate
ABC-Stockout Exposure
High: Revenue risk is top-tier. Demand swings raise the probability of a stockout during a peak period, amplifying the cost of any record inaccuracy.
Safety Stock
Moderate to High: Safety stock must buffer both demand variability and lead time variance. Reorder points are less reliable than AX and require closer monitoring.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
Moderate: Swing in movement gives errors partial cover. A discrepancy can look like a normal demand fluctuation and go undetected until a count is performed.
Scenario
A top SKU averages 300 units/week but spikes to 450 during promotions. A mispick error of 40 units goes unrecorded. During a promotional spike, replenishment was sized on inflated records. The team runs short at the worst possible moment, peak demand on a high-revenue item.
AZ
Count: Weekly
A-High Revenue Z-Erratic
ABC-Stockout Exposure
Highest: Top-line revenue at risk with no predictable buffer. A stockout on an AZ item triggers premium freight and emergency orders at exactly the moment demand is already unpredictable.
Safety Stock
Highest Required: Must buffer wide demand swings and lead time variance simultaneously. Safety stock alone cannot compensate for record inaccuracy; both levers must work correctly.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
Highest: No consistent movement baseline. Errors from mispicks, receiving mistakes, or unrecorded damage blend into normal volatility and compound across weeks undetected.
Scenario
System shows 50 units on hand. Actual count is 20. Errors compounded over six weeks of erratic movement. Replenishment decision is made on 50. Demand spikes. The team stocks out not because demand was unforeseeable, but because they acted on a bad record. Emergency order at premium freight follows.
BX
Count: Quarterly
B-Mid Revenue X-Stable
ABC-Stockout Exposure
Moderate: A stockout is a real margin hit but not a critical revenue event. BX items rarely trigger emergency orders because the pattern is stable enough to catch gaps early.
Safety Stock
Low to Moderate: Stable demand means safety stock is sized conservatively. Lead time variance is the primary buffer concern, not demand swings.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
Low: Stable movement makes discrepancies visible quickly. Errors are caught through normal operations before they affect a replenishment decision.
Scenario
A mid-tier SKU moves 150 units/week reliably. An entry error leaves 30 units unrecorded. Consistent movement makes the shelf look light before the next formal count. No replenishment decision is affected. Financial impact of any brief shortfall is real but manageable.
BY
Count: Quarterly
B-Mid Revenue Y-Moderate
ABC-Stockout Exposure
Moderate: Revenue impact of a stockout is real but not critical. Demand swings increase the probability of a short during a peak period, even if the financial consequence is bounded.
Safety Stock
Moderate: Safety stock must account for both demand variability and lead time. Reorder points are less reliable than BX and warrant closer monitoring during demand peaks.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
Moderate: Demand swings give errors partial cover. A discrepancy can look like a normal fluctuation and go undetected until a formal count is performed, especially during slow periods.
Scenario
A mid-tier seasonal SKU swings between 100-250 units/week. A receiving error of 40 units goes unnoticed during a slow stretch. When demand picks up, replenishment was sized on inflated records. Team runs short but not critically; safety stock partially absorbs the gap.
BZ
Count: Monthly
B-Mid Revenue Z-Erratic
ABC-Stockout Exposure
Moderate: Individual stockouts are manageable, but BZ items that consistently have record errors quietly erode service levels across the catalog without triggering obvious financial alarms.
Safety Stock
High Relative to Revenue Contribution: Erratic demand requires a larger buffer, but the revenue generated may not justify the carrying cost. A candidate for safety stock rationalization review.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
High: No consistent movement baseline. Errors accumulate and blend into volatility noise. Compounding errors across multiple BZ SKUs can produce a meaningful aggregate impact on fill rate.
Scenario
A mid-tier SKU with erratic demand (0-300 units/week) accumulates record errors over six weeks between counts. System shows 180, actual is 90. Replenishment decision is made on 180. When demand spikes, the team is short. Not a single catastrophic event, but repeated across multiple BZ SKUs it quietly degrades overall fill rate.
CX
Count: Semi-Annual
C-Low Revenue X-Stable
ABC-Stockout Exposure
Minimal: A stockout on a C item has negligible revenue impact. The cost of a formal count may exceed the cost of a stockout on this SKU.
Safety Stock
Minimal: Stable, low-velocity demand requires very little buffer. Replenishment is straightforward and lead time variance is manageable without large reserves.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
Lowest: Stable movement and low volume make discrepancies easy to spot and cheap to absorb. Errors rarely compound, and even when they do, the financial consequence is negligible.
Scenario
A low-velocity filler SKU moves 20 units/week. A mispick error goes unrecorded. At the next semi-annual count, the discrepancy is caught and corrected with no meaningful financial impact. The cost of counting more frequently would exceed any recoverable value.
CY
Count: Semi-Annual
C-Low Revenue Y-Moderate
ABC-Stockout Exposure
Low: Demand swings raise the probability of a short during peak periods, but the revenue consequence remains small. Stockouts here rarely warrant emergency corrective action.
Safety Stock
Low to Moderate: Some buffer is needed for demand swings, but carrying cost should be kept lean given the low revenue contribution. Over-stocking CY items is a common hidden cost.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
Low to Moderate: Demand swings can give small errors partial cover, but low transaction volume limits how much can compound. Financial consequence of undetected errors remains minimal.
Scenario
A low-revenue SKU with seasonal patterns swings from 15 to 80 units/week during peak. A small receiving error accumulates. A brief stockout occurs during peak. The revenue impact is negligible and the event goes unnoticed at the P&L level. Carrying cost of excess safety stock likely exceeds the stockout cost.
CZ
Count: Quarterly
C-Low Revenue Z-Erratic
ABC-Stockout Exposure
Minimal: Revenue consequence of a stockout is negligible. The primary risk is fulfillment SLA or customer experience, not margin. CZ items are common SKU rationalization candidates.
Safety Stock
Disproportionately High Relative to Revenue: Erratic demand forces a larger buffer that the revenue contribution cannot justify. Carrying cost and count effort on CZ items often exceed the value they generate.
XYZ-Record Accuracy Risk
High Concealment, Low Consequence: Errors hide longest on Z-tier SKUs, but on a C item the financial impact of compounding errors is negligible. Count resources are better allocated up the matrix.
Scenario
A low-revenue SKU with erratic movement, often quiet for several weeks then short bursts of picks, accumulates unrecorded mispicks and receiving gaps across the full quarterly interval between counts. System shows 35 on hand; physical count finds 10. Replenishment was sized on the inflated record. When demand spikes briefly, the team is short. Stockout cost is immaterial and does not register at the P&L level. Carrying cost, safety stock, and count effort together typically outweigh this SKU's revenue contribution, a common rationalization signal.
